Estate Planning Documents Checklist

A plain-English overview of common documents to gather and discuss with a qualified professional.

Updated June 16, 2026 · General U.S. educational information

Estate planning often involves more than a single will. The purpose of this guide is to help you recognize common document categories so you can ask better questions and gather existing paperwork.

Last will and testament

A will typically records who should receive certain property, who should serve as executor or personal representative, and who you would nominate as guardian for minor children. State law controls execution requirements and probate procedures.

Trust documents

A trust is a separate planning tool that may be used for privacy, management, incapacity planning, minor beneficiaries, or other goals. Trust planning can be technical, so avoid assuming a trust is necessary or unnecessary without advice.

Durable power of attorney

A power of attorney can authorize someone to act for you during life. Because it can grant significant authority, it should be prepared carefully and reviewed with a qualified professional.

Health care directive

Health care directives, medical powers of attorney, living wills, and related forms vary by state. These documents can help communicate medical decision-makers and preferences if you cannot speak for yourself.

Beneficiary designation records

Retirement accounts, life insurance, payable-on-death accounts, transfer-on-death registrations, and some workplace benefits may transfer through beneficiary forms rather than through a will. Keep a current list of where these forms are located.

Letter of instruction

A letter of instruction can collect practical information: contacts, account locations, memorial preferences, pet care notes, and digital asset instructions. It should not be used as a substitute for legally required documents.

Document organization checklist

  • Collect existing documents in one secure location.
  • Record where originals are stored.
  • List who has copies.
  • Review names, addresses, and beneficiary references for outdated information.
  • Ask a professional whether older documents still match current law and your current goals.

FAQ

Do I need every estate planning document listed here?

Not necessarily. The right documents depend on your state, assets, family, health, and goals.

Is a will enough by itself?

Sometimes, but not always. A will is one document in a broader plan. Other documents can address incapacity, health care, beneficiary designations, and asset transfer methods.

Where should documents be stored?

Use a secure place that trusted people can locate when needed. Avoid locking away the only copy where no one can access it.
