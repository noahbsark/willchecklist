Will Preparation Checklist

Gather the information most people need before creating or updating a will.

Updated June 16, 2026 · General U.S. educational information

A will preparation checklist is not a legal document. It is a structured way to gather information so you can make clearer decisions when you use an online service, meet an estate planning attorney, or update older paperwork.

Legal note: Signing, witnessing, notarization, property, guardianship, and probate rules vary by state. Use this checklist for organization, then confirm legal requirements with a qualified professional.

1. List the people involved

  • Your legal name and contact information.
  • Spouse or partner information, if applicable.
  • Children, dependents, and anyone financially reliant on you.
  • Potential executor or personal representative choices.
  • Backup executor choices.
  • Potential guardians for minor children or dependents.
  • Key advisors: attorney, accountant, financial professional, insurance contact, employer benefits contact.

2. Create a property snapshot

You do not need perfect values to begin. Start with categories and where records are kept.

  • Home, land, vehicles, and titled property.
  • Bank, brokerage, retirement, and investment accounts.
  • Life insurance policies and workplace benefits.
  • Business interests, royalties, or side-business assets.
  • Personal property with financial or emotional value.
  • Pets and pet care preferences.

3. List debts and recurring obligations

A clear list helps your executor know what exists. Include mortgages, loans, credit cards, subscriptions, taxes, storage units, and recurring bills. Do not publish sensitive account numbers in a shared document.

4. Review beneficiary designations

Some assets may pass by beneficiary designation rather than through a will. List each account that has a beneficiary field and note when you last reviewed it. For more detail, use the beneficiary checklist.

5. Find existing documents

  • Current will or codicils.
  • Trust documents, if any.
  • Durable power of attorney.
  • Health care directive or medical power of attorney.
  • Property deeds, titles, and insurance policies.
  • Marriage, divorce, adoption, or custody documents.

6. Draft your planning notes

Write plain-language notes about gifts, guardianship preferences, funeral or memorial preferences, digital accounts, and people who should be contacted. These notes are not a substitute for a valid will, but they can make your professional conversation more productive.

7. Choose your next step

If your situation is simple, an online will-making service may be worth comparing. If you have a blended family, minor children, property in multiple states, business ownership, special needs concerns, conflict, significant assets, or tax questions, consider speaking with a licensed attorney.

FAQ

Is this checklist the same as a will?

No. It is an organizer that helps you gather information before creating or updating legal documents.

Can I use this instead of an attorney?

This site does not provide legal advice. A licensed attorney can evaluate your state rules, family situation, assets, and signing requirements.

What should I do first?

Start with people, property, accounts, debts, beneficiary designations, and the location of important documents.
