Plain English

Estate planning glossary without the overwhelm.

Use these cautious definitions to understand conversations and checklists. The glossary is educational only and does not replace state-specific legal advice.

Important: legal terms can change meaning based on state law and document wording. Use this glossary to prepare better questions, not to decide what your documents should say.

Advance directive

A broad term often used for documents that explain health care preferences or name someone to help with health care decisions. Names and rules vary by state.

Beneficiary

A person or organization named to receive assets. Some assets pass by beneficiary designation outside a will.

Codicil

A document used to amend an existing will. Ask a qualified professional before relying on one because requirements can vary.

Durable power of attorney

A document that may let someone handle certain financial matters if you cannot. The exact authority depends on the document and state law.

Estate

The property, accounts, and obligations a person leaves behind. Some items may pass outside probate.

Executor

A person named to carry out instructions in a will and handle estate administration, subject to court and state rules.

Guardian

A person who may be nominated to care for minor children or dependents. Courts and state rules matter.

Intestacy

The situation where someone dies without a valid will for property that does not otherwise transfer.

Letter of instruction

A non-binding organizational note that can help loved ones locate documents and understand practical wishes.

Living trust

A trust created during life. It can be part of estate planning, but it is not automatically better for every person.

Personal representative

A term some states use for the person handling estate administration. It may overlap with executor or administrator.

Probate

A court-supervised process for administering certain assets after death. Not every asset necessarily goes through probate.

Self-proving affidavit

A separate statement used in some states to make probate easier. Requirements vary and should be confirmed locally.

Trust

A legal arrangement where property is managed according to trust terms. Trust planning can be complex and should be reviewed professionally.

Will

A legal document that can direct certain property and nominate roles after death. Validity requirements vary by state.
